Theorized multiple episodes of interbreeding between Neanderthal and modern humans

Substantial evidence and testing aims to undermine the idea that Neanderthals and modern humans only mixed once

Agence France-Presse in Paris
Wed 6 Sep 2023 11.25 EDTLast modified on Wed 6 Sep 2023 18.18 EDT

In a study recently published on Nature.com, Fernando A. Villanea & Joshua G. Schraiber researchers delved into the history of inbreeding between modern humans and Neanderthals. Going in, they knew that there had been at least one episode of interbreeding, but they sought to prove that there had been more than one. The study used data from the 1000 Genomes project and was able to support the theory that there were multiple interbreeding instances effectively. They concluded that every population except those from Africa have Neanderthal genes, but Eastern Asians have more said genes than Europeans.

It is widely agreed that when modern humans left Africa, around 60,000 years ago, they found and made offspring with Neanderthals. Supporting this idea are the remains of Ust’-Ishim, a sort of in-between human.

A big question is why Eastern Asians have more Neanderthal Ancestry than Europeans. Some think it’s because there was a single instance of mass interbreeding and then some evolutionary pressure cemented parts of the Neanderthal genome into modern populations. A theory as to why Europeans don’t have as much Neanderthal DNA is that after the aforementioned mixing happened, the modern European population interbred with a separate population that never encountered the Neanderthals as they had migrated out of Africa separately.

Additional remains of ancient individuals such as the ones from Peştera cu Oase indicate that there were additional instances of interbreeding, albeit smaller instances. The researchers found this rather annoying as it just further complicates the current theories on what happened.

In this article, we will be discussing the reason(s) for this discrepancy between the amount of Neanderthal genes present in Europeans and East Asians.

Using data from the 1000 Genomes Project, they analyzed Neanderthal fragments and they found that the average proportion of Neanderthal ancestry was 0.0137 in Europeans and 0.0164 in East Asians.

We developed a theory to try to understand our data and analysis and we decided on the idea that there had been multiple episodes of interbreeding (most likely 2), not just 2 episodes like we had previously thought. Both of our models supported this idea with a goodness-to-fit test not being able to reject the 2-episode idea, but rejecting the 1-episode idea.

In order to understand how Neanderthal genes have influenced different populations in Europe and Asia, we had challenges in individually analyzing each population. To get around this, we used a Computer Science technique called machine learning, which is useful in predicting things even without all of the information present. This method is incredibly useful when dealing with dense genetic data, too complex for a person to interpret. We trained the computer to learn patterns and make predictions about human and Neanderthal interactions. This approach, called deep learning, is great because it can make predictions even with incomplete data. Deep learning makes the most of what it has, allowing us to understand what isn’t immediately clear.

We used more computers to model and simulate Neanderthal and modern human interbreeding events. We trained a neural network to be able to recognize certain demographic models that each represented different levels/scenarios of interbreeding. It was able to correctly identify the model 58% of the time. This is significantly better than just randomly guessing as it would have a success rate of only about 20%. The neural network helped further corroborate the idea that there was more than one instant of interbreeding when we put in the “real” model, the one made from the Human Genome project.

After putting our data through a sophisticated computer model (FCNN), we were able to further support our hypothesis that more than one episode of interbreeding happened. The results were very interesting, saying that the FCNN supported our demographic models, which indicates that a model involving 3 episodes of interbreeding is more likely, or that a model with dilution is less likely, but still plausible (with probabilities of around 55% and 44% respectively). Our findings remain true even when we modify specific procedures and look for potential sources of error in our analysis. Moreover, this helps support our other models and their findings, making us almost completely sure that there was more than one instance of interbreeding.
